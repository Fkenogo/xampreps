## Q1

**Question:** Rose crossed the road as __________ as it was clear.

**Picked Answer:** soon

**Other Acceptable Answers:** immediately (less precise), quickly (not best fit)

**Tutor Dialogue** Tutor: Let’s read this carefully. “Rose crossed the road as ___ as it was clear.” What structure do you see? Student: “as … as” Tutor: Good. Now this structure is often used with time expressions. What is the full idea here? Student: She crossed when it became clear. Tutor: Exactly. So we need a word that means “immediately after.” What fits? Student: soon. Tutor: Yes. “as soon as it was clear” means she did not delay at all. Tutor: Why not “quickly”? Student: Because it doesn’t fit the structure. Tutor: Correct. The correct expression is **as soon as**.

## Q2

**Question:** Adam is two years __________ than his little sister.

**Picked Answer:** older

**Other Acceptable Answers:** elder (incorrect in this structure)

**Tutor Dialogue** Tutor: This sentence is comparing Adam and his sister. What word tells you that? Student: “than” Tutor: Exactly. “Than” signals comparison. So what form do we need? Student: A comparative form. Tutor: Correct. Now we have two choices many students confuse: “older” and “elder.” Which one works with “than”? Student: older. Tutor: Right. “Older than” is correct. “Elder” is usually used without “than,” for example: “my elder brother.” Tutor: So the correct answer is **older**.

## Q3

**Question:** The last bus leaves __________ Kampala at 12 noon.

**Picked Answer:** for

**Other Acceptable Answers:** to (not best fit in this structure)

**Tutor Dialogue** Tutor: Let’s think about direction. Is the bus going to Kampala or leaving Kampala? Student: It is going to Kampala. Tutor: Good. When something is going towards a place, which preposition do we use with “leave”? Student: for? Tutor: Exactly. We say “leaves for Kampala.” Tutor: What would “leaves Kampala” mean? Student: That it is leaving from Kampala. Tutor: Correct. So to show destination, we use **for**.

## Q31

**Question:** Turkeys are bigger than cocks.

**Instruction:** Rewrite the sentence using: **…as……..as…**

**Picked Answer:** Cocks are not as big as turkeys.

**Other Acceptable Answers:** A cock is not as big as a turkey.

**Less preferred:** Cocks are not as bigger as turkeys. (wrong structure)

**Tutor Dialogue** Tutor: Let’s first understand the original sentence. Who is bigger? Student: Turkeys are bigger than cocks. Tutor: Correct. Now we must use the pattern **as…as**. When two things are not equal, what do we usually add? Student: “Not as…as” Tutor: Exactly. So instead of saying “Turkeys are bigger than cocks,” we turn it around and say the smaller one is **not as big as** the larger one. Tutor: Which is the smaller one here? Student: Cocks. Tutor: Good. So the answer becomes? Student: Cocks are not as big as turkeys. Tutor: Perfect. Notice that after **as**, we use the adjective in its basic form: **big**, not **bigger**.
